Living in the first mile of a product

On the messy, generative space before a product has a roadmap, a team, or any proof it can be done — and why I keep choosing it.

Every product I've loved building started the same way: as a sentence nobody could yet prove. Not a spec, not a deck — a hunch with a pulse. The first mile is where that hunch is most fragile and most alive, and it's the part of the work I'd never give up.

Most of the craft people celebrate happens later — the scaling, the polish, the org charts. That work matters. But the decisions that actually shape a product are made in the fog at the start, when you have the least information and the most leverage. Get the first mile right and everything downstream gets easier. Get it wrong and no amount of execution saves you.

Scope by subtraction

The instinct in ambiguity is to add — more features, more research, more certainty before you commit. I've learned to do the opposite. Find the one thing the product has to be true about, and cut everything that isn't in service of it. A first version should be embarrassing in its narrowness and undeniable in its point.

Ship the version that's small enough to be wrong quickly, and sharp enough that being wrong teaches you something.

With hardware this discipline is brutal and clarifying. You can't fake a sensor reading or hand-wave a latency budget. The physical world tells you immediately whether your hunch survives contact with reality — and that feedback, as humbling as it is, is the fastest way I know to learn what's actually true.

Build the team the product needs

The other half of the first mile is people. A 0→1 product doesn't need a big team; it needs a small, sharp one where the seams between disciplines disappear. The firmware person who cares how it feels in the hand. The designer who reads a datasheet. I spend an outsized amount of energy here, because the team you assemble in the first mile is the product's real architecture.

None of this is comfortable. The first mile is where you're most likely to be wrong in public. But it's also where vision and reality are closest together — close enough that, with the right small team and enough conviction, you can reach across and pull the future a little nearer. That's the work. I wouldn't trade it.
